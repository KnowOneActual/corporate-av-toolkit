================================================================================
                          TELEPROMPTER SCRIPT
              "WHY YOU SHOULD LISTEN TO THE PRESENTER"
================================================================================
Instructions for Teleprompter Software:
1. Spoken text is in regular casing with frequent line breaks for easy scrolling.
2. Direct presenter cues, actions, and slide transitions are in brackets [LIKE THIS] 
   and should NOT be read aloud.
3. Slide breaks are marked with [--- SLIDE X ---].
================================================================================

[--- SLIDE 1: TITLE SLIDE ---]
[CUE: Start speaking immediately. Establish baseline microphone level.]

Good morning, everyone. 

Or good afternoon, depending on what timezone my brain is currently in.

Welcome. 

Today, we are going to explore one of the greatest mysteries in human history: 

"Why should anyone listen to the presenter?"

Now, while you ponder this profound question, you might notice that my slides look clean and my voice is projecting nicely.

That is because this is actually a stealthy five-minute audio-visual technical check.

So if I'm too quiet, or if my face looks green on screen, we'll fix it now!

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 2: THE CREDIBILITY FORMULA ---]
[CUE: Gesture to the formula box on screen.]

After years of intensive research, we have synthesized the Universal Credibility Formula.

As you can see, credibility is directly proportional to confidence and volume...

And it is inversely proportional to the number of times I say "Um."

Confidence is my job.

Volume is the tech booth's job.

And "Um" is the friction.

If my volume drops, that's a soundboard calibration issue.

If I say "Um," that's a personal calibration issue.

So we are working in partnership here!

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 3: THE CONFIDENCE MONITOR ---]
[CUE: Look down at the floor screen, acknowledging its position.]

This brings us to the confidence monitor on the floor in front of me.

The expectation is that I look at you, and only glance down occasionally.

The reality is that this screen is highly magnetic.

It takes enormous strength not to just stare at my feet and read this word-for-word like a hostage.

Let's do a live calibration. 

Tech team: the monitor looks great, the text size is spot on, and I am going to try my best to look up at the audience now.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 4: THE MICROPHONE PARADOX ---]
[CUE: Change vocal dynamics here. Speak softly, then speak with higher projection.]

Now for audio check part two: The Microphone Paradox.

When handed a mic, everyone wants to tap it.

Please don't. The sound engineers will wince.

Instead, let's test dynamic range.

I'm going to whisper a bit to see if you can still hear me clearly. 

[SPEAK IN A WHISPER]
Testing the noise gate. Can you still hear me in the back? Excellent.

[SPEAK IN NORMAL HIGH-ENERGY PRESENTING VOICE]
Now, I'll speak at a higher projection to make sure we aren't clipping the master channel.

Perfect. The sound is crisp and there's no feedback.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 5: PROJECTOR CALIBRATION ---]
[CUE: Gesture generally to the screen colors behind you.]

Next, we have a visual calibration check.

We have eight color swatches lined up.

These test the projector's color fidelity.

If my magenta block looks red, or the yellow looks like green...

Then either the projector bulb is tired, or my design choices are even worse than my wardrobe.

Everything looks highly vibrant up here. 

The colors are perfectly balanced.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 6: THE CLICKER LATENCY ---]
[CUE: Walk to stage left, press clicker, then walk to stage right.]

Let's test the wireless clicker range.

I'm going to walk over to stage left, press the button... and it works.

Now, stage right... press... works.

The latency check is successful.

We want to avoid the Clicker Paradox...

Where you press the clicker, nothing happens for three seconds, you press it again in a panic...

And suddenly you're three slides ahead, looking at your thank you slide before you've even introduced your topic.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 7: THE LASER POINTER ---]
[CUE: Turn on laser pointer and target a data point on the screen.]

Pillar number one of presentation authority is the laser pointer.

It is scientifically proven that humans have a cat-like response to a tiny dot of light.

If I point it here, you look here.

However, the most important rule is the danger zone.

Never point your laser pointer at the tech booth.

The crew has controls that can instantly cut your mic, play exit music, or turn off the projector.

Keep the laser on the screen!

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 8: THE VIDEO EMBED GAMBLE ---]
[CUE: Trace the flowchart boxes with your hand.]

The ultimate high-stakes gamble in modern presenting is the embedded video.

You click play, you wait, and you pray to the AV gods.

Usually, the video works, but there is no sound.

You look at the tech crew, they look at you, and you both participate in an awkward staring contest.

Thankfully, we are keeping it safe today with static slides.

But if you have a video in your presentation, make sure to test it during soundcheck!

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 9: PRESENTER WARDROBE ---]
[CUE: Look down at your outfit, then back at the audience.]

Pillar number two of credibility is your wardrobe.

It is a technical check for the video team.

If you wear a black shirt in front of a dark slide, on camera you look like a floating head.

If you wear tiny pinstripes, you create a moiré effect that makes the camera sensor look like it's hallucinating.

Also, always wear something with a waistband.

If you don't, the tech crew has to clip a heavy wireless battery pack to your collar...

Which ruins your posture and your confidence.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 10: STAGE BOUNDARIES ---]
[CUE: Walk center-stage, then walk left, then walk right.]

This slide is a physical boundary check.

Stage Left is a Safe Zone—nice lighting, no feedback.

Center Stage is the Spotlight, where the camera is framed.

And Stage Right... well, that is where we get close to the main speakers.

Let's test this.

Walking center...

Now walking right...

If we hear feedback, we know the boundary limit.

Everything sounds great. 

Tech crew, you tuned these feedback suppressors beautifully!

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 11: AUDIENCE ATTENTION ---]
[CUE: Point to the bar chart data.]

Let's look at the scientific data regarding audience attention.

As this bar chart shows, only ten percent of you are actually listening.

A whopping forty-eight percent are thinking of the lunch buffet.

And thirty percent are checking emails.

This is why visual engagement is so important—it is the only way I can compete with your inbox!

Also, tech crew: check the text at the very bottom of the screen.

If you can read that margin calibration notice, our projector framing is perfect.

[CUE: ADVANCE TO NEXT SLIDE]



[--- SLIDE 12: TRUST THE TECH CREW ---]
[CUE: Prepare to transition mic. Raise energy level.]

In conclusion, the ultimate rule of presentation credibility isn't your slides, your laser pointer, or your wardrobe.

It's the technical team in the back of the room.

They hold the ultimate power to make you look like a rockstar, or turn you into a muted mime.

Let's give a warm round of applause to our AV and technical crew...

And let's get the main event started.

Thank you!

[CUE: MIC OFF. EXIT STAGE. OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN.]
